FINANCIAL GROWTH
IN MALAYSIA: A Retrospective
Since it's inception, Malaysia has been gradually climbing the economical ladder through industry booms across many years.
From its early reliance on agriculture and natural resources to becoming a major player in manufacturing and services, the country has steadily diversified its economy.
As we explore the nation's financial journey, we’ll examine the factors that have shaped its current economic landscape.
POVERTY RATE CHANGE
in selected states
From the 1980s to 2020, Significant decline in poverty rates was shown across Malaysian states like Johor, Selangor, Kuala Lumpur, and Pulau Pinang, reflecting the country’s economic growth and social policies.
However, the spike around 2020 marks the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic, which temporarily reversed progress.
This highlights both the success in reducing poverty and the vulnerabilities exposed during crises, underscoring the need for inclusive growth strategies moving forward.
As the country continued its financial recovery, understanding how such events impact poverty ensures resilience that reaches all segments of society.
FINANCIAL RANKING
in Mean Income by States (2002-2022)

Looking at the mean income rankings of Malaysian states over 20 years, key trends in economic growth and regional disparities are shown.

Top Performers

  • Putrajaya, Kuala Lumpur, and Selangor dominate the rankings, driven by urbanization, infrastructure, and diverse industries.

Mid-Level States

  • Johor has consistently ranked 4th, benefiting from industrial growth and proximity to Singapore.

Lower-Ranked States

  • States like Terengganu, Negeri Sembilan, and Perak show more variability, reflecting less diversified economies reliant on agriculture or natural resources.

Regional income disparities plays a huge factor in deciding mean income, highlighting the need for balanced economic development to support growth across all states.

GDP GROWTH PER YEAR
by Sector (2016-2023)
  • Every sector faced a sharp decline in 2020 due to the pandemic, followed by recovery.
  • Construction experienced significant volatility, with mining hitting a -19.317% dip in 2020.
  • Manufacturing and Services showed resilience, recovering quickly after 2020, driven by global trade and digital economy growth.
  • Construction was highly volatile, particularly impacted by pandemic disruptions.
  • Import Duties spiked in 2017, reflecting changes in trade policies or tariffs.

Malaysia has diversified their economy to ensure sustainable growth, particularly by supporting strong sectors like services and manufacturing while addressing vulnerabilities in mining and construction.
